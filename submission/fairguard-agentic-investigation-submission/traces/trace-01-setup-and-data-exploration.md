What this competition actually rewards
Reading the details page carefully, two things stand out:

1. The findings bar is binary, not a score. Panelists spot-check your findings first. If they don't hold up as real journalism — sourced, accurate, public interest — you're not ranked at all, regardless of how good your skill is. So the strategy isn't "build the coolest agent." 2. The skill is what wins. Once you clear the findings bar, you're scored on four equal-weight dimensions:

Keeps the investigation organized (long-running state)
Efficient with the corpus (push work to deterministic tools, save the LLM for reasoning)
Human-verifiable (provenance, citations, audit trail)
Extends what an agent can do (entity resolution, cross-record matching, domain parsers)
And reproducibility is a gate — if a panelist can't re-run your skill, you cap at 1/3 on every dimension. This is huge. Many teams will lose points here without realizing it.

Fair — I jumped ahead. Let me actually explain what this is, in plain terms.

What the challenge is
Northwestern's journalism school is running a competition to see if AI agents can do real investigative journalism. They're giving every team the same big pile of government records and asking: can you find something newsworthy in there using AI, and can you package the way you found it so other journalists could use the same approach on their own investigations?

You're competing against journalists, newsroom developers, students, and civic tech people. Top three teams win cash ($5K / $2.5K / $1K) and get their work featured on the GAIN blog. Deadline is July 15.

What the data is
Two kinds of government records, both public, covering 2022 through March 2026:

1. Lobbying filings (the bigger pile — about 8 GB)

When companies, trade groups, or foreign governments want to influence US policy, they hire lobbyists. By law, those lobbyists have to file paperwork saying:

Who's paying them (the client)
How much they're being paid
What issues they're lobbying on (healthcare, taxes, defense, etc.)
Who in government they're lobbying (which agencies, which committees)
Who the actual lobbyists are — including whether those lobbyists used to work in government
This data is split into Senate filings (cleaner, JSON format) and House filings (messier, 409,000 separate XML files). They overlap — the same lobbying engagement often shows up in both. Both come from official government sources.

2. Congressional press releases (about 500 MB)

Every member of Congress publishes press releases on their official websites — statements about bills they support, issues they care about, things happening in their district. The dataset has all of these scraped from house.gov and senate.gov sites. About 48,000 in 2025 alone.

The implicit story the data is set up to tell
The reason these two datasets are together is the obvious tension: what politicians say publicly vs. who's paying to influence them privately. A member of Congress might issue press releases about cracking down on Big Pharma while pharma companies are lobbying their committee. Or a member might suddenly start talking about cryptocurrency right when crypto firms ramp up lobbying spend.

The data lets you connect those dots. Press releases tell you what politicians say. Lobbying filings tell you who's paying to shape what they do. Cross-referencing the two is where the journalism lives.

What you have to actually submit
Four things:

1. An "Agent Skill" — this is the main thing. It's basically a packaged workflow for Claude Code: a folder with instructions, scripts, and reference material that teaches an AI agent how to do one specific investigative task well. Examples of the kind of skill they want:

A tool that cleans up messy company names so "Microsoft Corp" and "MICROSOFT CORPORATION" get recognized as the same entity
A workflow that keeps a long investigation organized across multiple days/sessions
A document review interface that makes it easy for a reporter to verify what the AI found
A parser for some specific kind of government document
The key thing: it has to be reusable. Not just for this dataset — for any future investigation a journalist might run. That's how you compete.

2. A findings report — what newsworthy stuff you actually discovered by running your skill on the data. Real journalism, not "here's what the data contains." Something a reporter would chase.

3. Interaction traces — logs of your conversations with the AI agent while you did the investigation. Inputs, outputs, where you stepped in as a human. Proof of how the work actually got done.

4. A README — a map of your submission tying everything together.

How they judge it
Two gates:

Gate 1 — are the findings real journalism? If the things you "found" aren't actually newsworthy, accurate, and traceable to specific records, you're out. They don't even score the rest. So you need at least one solid finding.

Gate 2 — is the skill useful? Once findings pass, they score the skill on four things, each equally weighted:

Does it keep a long investigation organized?
Is it efficient with a big corpus (not burning tokens on stuff a regular script could do)?
