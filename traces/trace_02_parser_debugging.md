## Step 1: What is lobbying?

In the US, if you're a company, an industry group, or even a foreign government and you want Congress or a federal agency to do something — pass a law, fund a program, write a rule a certain way — you hire people called **lobbyists** to advocate for you.

A lobbyist is someone who talks to senators, representatives, and government staffers on your behalf. They explain why your company wants X, they help write legislation, they push for funding in certain bills, they meet with agency officials.

This is legal. It's been legal forever. The First Amendment protects the right to "petition the government." But because it can obviously turn into corruption — a company pays a lobbyist, the lobbyist takes a senator to dinner, the senator votes the way the company wants — Congress passed a law called the **Lobbying Disclosure Act (LDA)**.

The LDA says: if you're being paid to lobby, you have to fill out paperwork every three months disclosing:

- Who's paying you (the "client")
- How much they're paying you
- What issues you're lobbying about
- Which parts of the government you're lobbying

All this paperwork is public. Anyone can read it. The Senate publishes its copy, the House publishes its copy. That paperwork is exactly what our dataset is.

## Step 2: The revolving door

Here's where it gets interesting.

Many lobbyists used to work *in* the government. They were senators, congressional staffers, agency officials. Then they leave government and join a lobbying firm.

Why? Because they know everyone. They know how the system works. They know which committee handles what. They know which staffer is the gatekeeper. That knowledge is *extremely* valuable to companies that want to influence policy.

This is called the **revolving door** — people rotating between government jobs and private-sector influence jobs. It's not illegal, but it has obvious conflict-of-interest implications:

- If you're a senator's chief of staff today, and tomorrow you're a lobbyist getting paid to influence that same senator, are you really independent?
- If you ran a government agency and made decisions affecting Company X, and then six months later Company X hires you, did your agency decisions secretly favor them?

To address the most obvious version of this, there's a **"cooling-off period"** — a federal rule that says senior officials can't immediately lobby their former agency after leaving. Senators have to wait 2 years. Senior executive-branch officials have to wait 1 year. There's also a permanent ban on lobbying about "particular matters" you personally worked on in government.

## Step 3: Now, Jim Bridenstine

Jim Bridenstine is the case study. Here's his story.

**2013–2018**: He's a Republican Congressman from Oklahoma. While in Congress, he serves on the House Science Subcommittee that oversees NASA.

**April 2018**: President Trump appoints him the 13th **Administrator of NASA** — the head of the agency. Top of the org chart. He runs everything.

**As Administrator (2018–2021)**, he launches and expands several major NASA initiatives:
- The **Artemis program** — NASA's plan to return humans to the Moon. Massive multi-billion-dollar program.
- **Commercial Lunar Payload Services (CLPS)** — a program where NASA pays private companies to deliver things to the Moon's surface.
- **Commercial LEO Destinations** — a program where NASA pays private companies to build space stations to replace the International Space Station.

These programs award contracts worth billions of dollars to private space companies. **Bridenstine personally helped design which companies could compete and what the rules would be.**

**January 2021**: Bridenstine steps down as NASA Administrator (new administration takes over).

**January 25, 2021 — five days later**: He gets hired as a senior advisor at Acorn Growth Companies, a private-equity firm focused on aerospace investments.

**April 2021**: He joins the board of directors at **Viasat** (a satellite company that does business with NASA and the Department of Defense).

**April 2021**: He also becomes the **chair of the advisory board at Voyager Space Holdings** — a company that was selected to build NASA's next space station under the *Commercial LEO Destinations program he had just expanded*.

**January 2024**: He joins the board of Starlab Space (Voyager's joint venture with Airbus).

For roughly 4 years (2021–2024), he is on multiple boards and advisory roles at companies that have business with NASA — but he's not yet a **registered lobbyist**, so he doesn't have to file LDA paperwork. He's just an advisor and board member. Legal. Disclosed. Normal in the industry.

**October 2024**: He founds a lobbying firm called **The Artemis Group, LLC**, based in Oklahoma.

Pause on that name. The firm is named after the NASA program *he himself launched as Administrator*. That's not subtle. It's like a former FDA Commissioner naming his pharmaceutical lobbying firm "The Approval Group."

**Q4 2024 onward**: The Artemis Group starts filing lobbying disclosures. The clients are all space companies. The lobbying targets are Congress and federal agencies — including **NASA itself**.

Specifically, in our dataset:
- The firm has filed 133 lobbying reports with the Senate so far
- 52 of those reports name NASA as one of the agencies they're lobbying
- 9 different client companies are paying The Artemis Group to lobby NASA on their behalf

## Step 4: Who else works at The Artemis Group?

This is the part that turns a one-person profile into a structural story.

When you look at *who else* is registered as a lobbyist at The Artemis Group, you find:

**Gabe Sherman** — was Bridenstine's *Chief of Staff at NASA* (the #2 person at the agency, basically). Before that, he was Bridenstine's District Director when Bridenstine was a Congressman. The two have worked together for 12+ years.

**Mark Piland** — was Bridenstine's *Chief of Staff in Congress*.

**Christopher Ingraham** — was Bridenstine's *Senior Policy Advisor in Congress*, and before that worked on the House Space Subcommittee.

**Kathryn Wall** — was *Chief of Staff of the National Space Council* (the White House body that coordinated space policy during Bridenstine's NASA tenure).

So the firm isn't just Bridenstine. It's Bridenstine plus four people who all worked under him in government, now all targeting NASA together as registered lobbyists.

## Step 5: Who are the clients?

The Artemis Group represents 27 client companies — all in space and aerospace. The relevant ones for the story:

**Voyager Space Holdings** — building Starlab, the NASA-funded space station. Bridenstine sat on Voyager's advisory board for 3+ years before becoming their registered lobbyist. Voyager's Starlab project was selected under the Commercial LEO Destinations program *Bridenstine expanded as Administrator*.

**Lunar Outpost** — won a NASA contract to build lunar rovers. That contract was under the CLPS program *Bridenstine personally established as Administrator*. Lunar Outpost's lobbying description: "Enabling lunar surface mobility."

**Redwire** — a major NASA contractor. Their lobbying filings explicitly say they want The Artemis Group to "Support Gateway Program" — referring to Gateway, *a flagship project of Bridenstine's Artemis program*.

**United Launch Alliance** — the Boeing/Lockheed joint venture that launches NASA rockets. Their filings name "Monitor NASA Authorization Act; Defense Appropriations; NDAA."

**Quantum Space** — a space company that was an Artemis Group client.

And then in **May 2026** (two weeks ago), the story took its latest twist: **Bridenstine left The Artemis Group to become CEO of Quantum Space** — a company that had been his lobbying client just months earlier.

So the arc is now:
1. He ran NASA (regulator)
2. He went on the boards of NASA-contractor companies (advisor)
3. He founded a firm to lobby NASA for those companies (lobbyist)
4. He took over as CEO of one of those companies (executive)

## Step 6: Is any of this illegal?

**Probably not.**

The cooling-off period — the rule that prohibits senior officials from lobbying their former agency for one year after leaving — expired in January 2022. Bridenstine waited until late 2024 to register as a lobbyist. He waited nearly four years. He was not legally required to wait that long.

There's a separate, permanent ban on lobbying about "particular matters" you were personally involved in. Proving a violation of that would require records of his exact official activities at NASA, which we don't have.

So this is the textbook example of a *legal* revolving-door pattern. Everything is disclosed. Everything follows the rules.

**But it's exactly the kind of pattern journalism investigates** — because legal doesn't mean unproblematic. The questions a reporter asks:

- Did the programs Bridenstine launched as Administrator (CLPS, Commercial LEO Destinations) benefit the companies that later hired him?
- Did his decisions as Administrator about which companies could compete shape his future client list?
- Does NASA still treat its former Administrator with deference when he comes back representing private clients?
- Is naming a lobbying firm after a program you launched ethically problematic, even if legal?

## Step 7: Why this matters as a finding

What's newsworthy isn't "Bridenstine is corrupt" — there's no evidence of that. What's newsworthy is the **structural pattern**:

- A former agency head founds a lobbying firm targeting his former agency
- He fills the firm with his former government staff (who also worked at the agency)
- His clients are companies whose business is shaped by programs he personally established
- He eventually becomes CEO of one of those clients

It's a clean documented example of the regulatory revolving door operating at full speed. And the data lets us prove every step of it with specific filings, dollar amounts, and names.
